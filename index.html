Winter Is Coming – and Some Neighborhoods are Better Prepared Than Others

Data shows impoverished, minority neighborhoods most likely to face issues with heating during the colder months.

By: Jarrod Barry
Dec. 23

As New York City braces for the throes of winter, data compiled from the city’s Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) shows that heat and hot water complaints are concentrated mostly in poorer neighborhoods.

311 records from January 1 to Dec. 1 this year show that neighborhoods in upper Manhattan and southwestern Bronx registered the most complaints regarding a lack of heat or hot water. These included Manhattan Community District 12, comprised of the Inwood and Washington Heights neighborhoods, and Bronx Community Districts 4, 5, and 7, which includes the neighborhoods of Highbridge, Fordham, and Morris Heights.

It’s unclear what accounts for the discrepancy between neighborhoods. Many of the neighborhoods with the most complaints are among the poorest in the city. For instance, the Bronx’s 5th and 4th CD’s have two of the highest rates of poverty in the city, according to the Department of Planning. Many of these districts also have large Black and Hispanic populations.

In January 2023, City Comptroller Brad Lander put out a report titled “Turn Up the Heat” examining heat and hot water complaints across the city. The report found many neighborhoods with heating violations “were subject to redlining in the late 1930s, which limited the ability for homeowners and landlords to receive low-cost, low-risk mortgages”. The report found that this lack of funding led to poor building maintenance and “problems of chronic disrepair”.

Caleb Smith is the resiliency coordinator at WE ACT, a community activist group that supports low-income tenants. While they couldn’t explain the heat complaints specifically, they said that these data are in line with other research on housing quality across the city. They added that the structural problems with buildings in these neighborhoods “have a strong correlation with systemic practices of disinvestment in communities of color, such as redlining.”

Smith pointed to a 2024 report put out by the Mayor’s Office of Climate & Environmental Justice that examined, among other things, housing maintenance issues. It found a similar racial component with poor housing conditions, including poor heating, noting that they “are more likely to occur in high poverty neighborhoods, and disproportionately impact Black and Hispanic or Latino families that rent their homes”.

These complaints come in the context of a slight increase in heat-related complaints this year. According to HPD, year-to-date heat complaints are up about 5% compared to last year’s heat season (a period from Oct 1 to March 31 when landlords are required to turn the heat on). So far this season, HPD has registered more than 4,000 additional complaints to 311.

Tenants who have no heat in their apartment have a number of steps they can take. In a 2023 article from The City, Andrea Shapiro, director of programs at the tenant advocacy group Met Council on Housing, recommends recording the temperature in the apartment using a thermometer. Residents should then report the problem to the landlord right away.

“The first day you don’t have heat, or the first time you notice there’s a problem with the heating system, tell someone — even if you know they’re not going to do anything,” she was quoted as saying in the article.

Caleb Smith was quick to note that access to heating is a right, not a privilege, and tenants should take action if it isn’t corrected quickly.

“All tenants in New York State are legally entitled to heat and hot water,” they said. If the problem isn’t fixed or happens persistently, tenants can “report the loss of heat and hot water to 311 to get a HPD inspector to come confirm the complaints and notify the landlord they will be fined if they don't remedy the issue.”
